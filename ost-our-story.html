Before the Table
Lagos, 2020

Cooking was a chore.
Then the world stopped.

I am the CEO of LifeBank, a global health company. Monday to Saturday, I run hard — logistics, governments, crises, lives. When COVID arrived in Lagos in 2020, we built drive-through screening centers while the governments were doing what governments do. I was inside those screening tents. Terrified. Exhausted. Holding it together for everyone else.

Before that year, cooking was something I did because it had to be done. It was never mine. It was never for me. It was just another task in an already impossible list.

Then the world forced me to slow down. No flights. No international trips. Just home — my husband, my kids, four walls, and a kitchen I had never really used as anything other than a passage.

The Discovery
Every Sunday

I needed to be
completely gone.

I started cooking on Sundays. Not Nigerian food — I already knew Nigerian food. It had to be hard. Interesting. Explorative. A country I had never cooked before, every single week. A full four-course dinner from somewhere entirely new.

It didn't have to be delicious. It had to allow me to be completely gone. When you are researching a Peruvian ceviche or trying to understand the chemistry of a Moroccan pastilla, you cannot think about oxygen concentrators or supply chains or government bureaucracy. You are just there — in the recipe, in the technique, in the smell of something unfamiliar becoming something real in your hands.

That disappearance was the most important thing I found in 2020. Not the food. The going away.

The Table
Minnesota, Now

Two years. Every country.
Then something bigger.

I cooked every Sunday for two years. Every country I could find a recipe for. I shared it on Instagram under #SundayDinner — not for followers, not for a brand. Just to mark that it happened. That I was there. That I disappeared into something beautiful for a few hours before the week started again.

Then we moved to the United States. The Sunday ritual broke. Life reassembled itself differently. But I knew I couldn't lose what I had found in that Lagos kitchen — that specific quality of joy, the kind that only comes when you are doing something difficult and interesting for no reason other than that it matters to you.

Our Sunday Table is the effort to get that back. To give those two years a permanent home. To bake at 5am on Sundays before the house wakes up, to teach the science behind every recipe, to honour the heritage in every ingredient — and to invite you to the table too.

The business grows only as fast as joy allows. That is the only rule I have ever had.

The Mission
Joy · Heritage · Science

A Nigerian American table,
set for the world.

I am Nigerian and American and I carry both cultures in everything I cook. West Africa is the largest cocoa-producing region on earth. Nigeria is the second-largest Guinness market in the world. These are not footnotes — they are the story. African ingredients belong on every table, not just ours.

Every recipe I publish carries three things: the joy of making something precise and beautiful on a Sunday morning, the heritage of wherever the recipe comes from, and the science of why it works. You will leave every post knowing something you did not know before. That is the promise.

And a portion of everything sold through this table goes directly to food security around the world — co-governed with this community. Because the table should feed everyone.

Temie Giwa-Tubosun
Founder · Our Sunday Table · CEO, LifeBank
